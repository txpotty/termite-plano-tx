Initial Termite Inspection

The first step in effective termite control is a comprehensive inspection. Our trained technicians will meticulously examine your property, both inside and out, looking for tell-tale signs of termite activity.

What We Look For During Inspection:

  • Mud tubes on walls or foundations
  • Swarmers (winged termites) or discarded wings, especially near windows and doors

  • Termite droppings (frass), which resemble sawdust or tiny pellets

A detailed inspection helps us identify the type of termite, the location of colonies, and the extent of the damage. This information is crucial for developing an effective treatment plan.

Common Termite Problems and Signs You Need Service

Recognizing the signs of a termite infestation early is crucial for minimizing damage and cost. Many homeowners are unaware they have a termite problem until the damage is significant. Here's a look at some common issues and what they might indicate:

Swarming Termites or Discarded Wings

One of the most visible signs of a termite infestation is the presence of swarmers, which are winged reproductive termites. These typically emerge in the spring or after a rain shower to mate and establish new colonies. Seeing swarmers, especially indoors, is a strong indication of an active infestation. Finding piles of discarded wings near windowsills, light fixtures, or in spiderwebs is also a clear sign that a termite swarm has occurred nearby. These winged termites are often mistaken for flying ants, but upon closer inspection, termites have straight antennae, a thick waist, and wings of equal length, while ants have elbowed antennae, a pinched waist, and unequal wing lengths. The presence of swarmers means that a mature termite colony is living within or near your home and is actively reproducing, making it an urgent situation that requires professional attention.

Mud Tubes on Walls or Foundation

Subterranean termites build tunnels, often referred to as mud tubes, from soil and wood particles. These tubes provide them with a protected pathway between their underground colonies and their food source – the wood in your home. You’ll typically find mud tubes on exterior and interior foundation walls, crawl spaces, basements, and even on plumbing pipes. These tubes are roughly the width of a pencil and serve as a visible indicator of termite foraging activity. The presence of mud tubes signifies that subterranean termites are present and actively accessing the wood in your structure, necessitating immediate inspection and treatment. Disturbing these tubes may reveal live termites inside, further confirming an active infestation.

Damaged or Hollow-Sounding Wood

Termites consume wood from the inside out, often leaving a thin veneer of paint or wood. This makes the damage difficult to detect from the surface. If you tap on wooden structures like doorframes, baseboards, or wall studs and they sound hollow or feel soft, it could be a sign of termite damage. Probing questionable areas with a screwdriver or knife may reveal galleries or tunnels carved by termites. This type of damage compromises the structural integrity of your home and indicates a significant, ongoing termite infestation that has been active for some time, requiring professional evaluation and treatment to prevent further destruction.

Termite Droppings (Frass)

Drywood termites, unlike subterranean termites, live entirely within the wood they infest and do not require contact with the soil. As they tunnel through the wood, they expel their waste, known as frass. Frass appears as tiny, hexagonal pellets that resemble sawdust or coffee grounds. You might find small piles of frass near or below termite-infested wood, often along with small holes in the surface of the wood from which the frass was pushed out. The presence of frass is a definitive sign of a drywood termite infestation and requires specialized treatment methods to eradicate the colony within the wood.

Tight-Fitting Doors or Windows

As termites consume wood, moisture levels within the damaged areas can increase, causing the wood to swell. This swelling can lead to doors and windows becoming difficult to open or close. While this issue can also be caused by normal humidity fluctuations, combined with other signs of termite activity, it can be an indicator that termites are actively damaging the wooden frames around your doors and windows. If you notice this problem alongside other potential termite signs, it's wise to have a professional inspection to determine the underlying cause and address any potential termite issues before they worsen and cause more significant structural problems.

Pinpoint Holes in Drywall or Other Surfaces

In some cases, termites can create small holes in drywall or wallpaper as they tunnel through the wood framing behind the finish. These holes are often tiny, about the size of a pinhead. While other things can cause small holes, if you see these in conjunction with other potential termite indicators, it's worth investigating further. These small openings can sometimes be exit holes for swarmers or openings used by termites to discard frass, depending on the species. Seeing these holes should prompt a professional inspection to determine if termites are active within your walls.
